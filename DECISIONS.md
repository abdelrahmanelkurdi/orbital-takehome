# Product decision: grounded citations & click-to-verify

## What the data showed

Beta analytics and interviews pointed to the same failure mode: users valued the product when answers tied to real document sections, but lost trust when the model sounded authoritative without support. Those are two different slices of feedback. Among **thumbs-up** events, **75 of 77** followed responses that cited at least one source — citations strongly correlated with satisfaction. Among **thumbs-down** events, the pattern was different: **17 of 25** still followed *cited* responses, which fits “it cited something that isn’t there” more than “it didn’t cite anything.” Interviews made the stakes explicit: partners called fabricated clauses on £40M deals “terrifying,” and one associate stopped using the tool after a fake section reference. One partner went further on willingness to pay: they would **pay double the licence fee** for honest uncertainty — *“A confidence indicator would change everything for me.”* That is a direct financial signal that visible grounding matters as much as raw answer quality.

## Why grounded citations over other options

I chose a **dual-agent trust layer** (stream the answer, then an independent judge + deterministic quote check) rather than workflow features (document library, Word export, side-by-side compare) or stronger answer-agent prompting alone. Workflow improvements address real friction but do not fix confidently wrong citations. Self-reported trust measurement from the answer model was ruled out: the model that wrote the answer cannot reliably grade its own homework. The judge classifies each paragraph as document-backed, mixed, general knowledge, or unsupported; a substring check marks whether the judge’s quote actually appears in extracted text, which powers green vs amber chips and PDF highlight on click. That stack targets the beta’s negative-feedback pattern while keeping streamed UX — users read the answer while “Checking sources…” runs for a few seconds after the stream ends. Per-block jump-to-quote links transform citations from decorative footers into something lawyers can verify in seconds.

## What I’d do next

With more time on this initiative, I would tighten quote extraction (a sequential fragment verifier or char-offset index at upload so fewer amber chips appear when the substance is right but the judge paraphrases), add `citation_clicked` and verified-quote analytics to measure whether the trust UX changes retention, and tune the answer agent to state uncertainty in prose as a complement to judge banners — not a replacement. The highest-impact item outside this deliverable is a **firm document library**: beta data showed ~76% of uploads were duplicates of files already in the product, and users asked for documents to persist across chats — a shared, firm-scoped store where a lease is uploaded once, but indexed for retrieval rather than attached wholesale to each new conversation (that would fill the context window immediately). At query time, embed the user's question, pull the ~10 most similar documents from the firm index, and answer over that subset — roughly the embeddings + RAG path in our multi-document design, optimized for production.
